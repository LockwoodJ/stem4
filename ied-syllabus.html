Course Description

Introduction to Engineering Design (IED) introduces students to the engineering design process, technical sketching, measurement, computer-aided design, prototyping, and documentation. Students will solve real-world problems through structured design challenges, performance tasks, and collaborative engineering projects.

This course emphasizes creativity, problem-solving, communication, persistence, and responsible use of engineering tools and technology.

Course Units

The course is organized into four major units that build from foundational design skills to more advanced modeling, product development, and mechanical motion.

Unit 1

Design and Problem Solving
Students learn the design process, engineering documentation, sketching, measurement, and problem-solving strategies.

Unit 2

Assembly Design
Students develop CAD skills, create parts and assemblies, and explore how components fit and function together.

Unit 3

Thoughtful Product Design
Students analyze user needs, improve products, and design with empathy, purpose, and usability in mind.

Unit 4

Making Things Move
Students investigate motion, mechanisms, and mechanical systems through hands-on design and modeling.

Students will complete the official PLTW End-of-Course (EOC) Exam at the conclusion of the course. The EOC Exam will count as 100 points toward the final course grade.

Late Work, Revisions & Make-Up Work

Late Work

Assignments submitted late will receive a 10% deduction for each day late.

Revisions

Tests and quizzes may not be revised. Classwork, design challenges, and performance tasks may be revised for additional credit.

Absences

Students receive one class period to make up work for each class period missed.

To be eligible for full credit through revision, the original submission must be turned in on time. Late initial submissions may still be accepted, but they are not eligible to earn full credit through revision.

Academic Integrity & Engineering Ethics

All student work must represent original effort and authentic design thinking. Engineering work depends on honesty, documentation, and the ability to explain how and why a solution was created.

Original Engineering Work

Students must create their own sketches, models, CAD files, documentation, and design explanations. Copying another student’s work or submitting work that is not your own is not permitted.

CAD Integrity

Students must be able to explain their design choices, modeling steps, measurements, and final submitted files. Outside inspiration must be credited appropriately.

AI Use Policy: AI tools may be used for brainstorming, idea generation, and research support. AI may not be used to generate complete solutions, complete designs, final CAD work, or submitted responses. Students are responsible for understanding and explaining all work they submit.

Course Philosophy

This course is designed to feel like an introduction to real engineering practice. Students will be challenged to think critically, work collaboratively, document carefully, and improve through feedback.

Success in this course is not based only on getting the “right answer.” Students are expected to apply the design process, learn from failure, revise their work, and communicate ideas clearly.
